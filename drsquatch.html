UX Framework

  • Problem
  • Solutions

Diagnosis

I didn't start with Figma. I started by walking the site.

My first weeks were a heuristic walkthrough of the full experience. I paired that with team conversations, a competitive audit of other DTC personal care brands, and scroll depth data from Hotjar.

The pattern was clear. Users were landing, engaging, and stopping. Not bouncing. Stopping. Traffic wasn't the problem. What happened after the click was.

Two issues kept surfacing.

No path forward. Pages were built in isolation. Users who drifted off the critical path hit dead ends with nowhere to go. Discoverability was accidental.

Not enough to decide. Dr. Squatch sells a product that needs a little convincing. But the right information wasn't showing up at the right moments. Users hit decision points without enough confidence to act.

These weren't isolated UX problems. They were symptoms of a site that had scaled without a connective framework underneath it.

Solutions

To address both the structural and informational gaps I'd diagnosed, I developed two UX frameworks that would work in tandem across the Dr. Squatch digital experience.

Driftwood established intentional entry and exit points throughout the site, ensuring users always had a clear path forward and eliminating dead ends that were quietly killing discoverability and retention.

Lighthouse brought the right information to the surface at the right moments, strategically placing product education and trust signals where users were most likely to need them before making a purchase decision.

Together they gave the experience a connective backbone it previously lacked, designed equally for the user who wants to feel guided, and the business that needs them to convert.

Lighthouse
